# Primitive Records

Primitive records are durable Ready package inputs. They are the units a
product orchestrator needs to build, prove, operate, or govern the intended
product package from the Ready tree.

Primitive records use:

```yaml
kind: primitive
class: premise # premise | intent | standard | service | artifact
type: belief  # subtype valid for the class
compiler_role: source_truth
```

`class` names the primitive family. `type` names the narrower subtype inside
that family. `compiler_role` tells compilers and orchestrators how to treat the
record. The valid values live in
[`vocabulary.yaml`](../../vocabulary.yaml). Do not use `primitive_class`,
`fields.premise_type`, or `fields.intent_type`.

Humans normally read compiled views in a consuming app or docs; the raw file is
optimized for parsing, validation, diffing, and merge review.

## Primitive Classes

- `premise`: why the product or feature should exist.
- `intent`: what the product promises to do.
- `standard`: how the product must be built, measured, judged, or maintained.
- `service`: what the product depends on to build, prove, run, or monitor.
- `artifact`: durable supporting content such as customer statements, source
  documents, proof corpora, snippets, designs, and manifests.

Flags are not primitives. Governance, settings, and manifest records are also
not primitives. They can be important Ready records, but they do not state
durable product package inputs in the same way.

Compiler role still matters. A premise states product reasoning, an intent
states product promise, a proof-corpus artifact may be regression evidence, and
a service may be a build or runtime dependency. Tools should read `kind`,
`class`, `type`, refs, path, and schema before deciding how a record compiles.

Status controls package compilation:

- `ready` primitive records are included in normal package compilation as
  current intended product truth.
- `draft` records make the package incomplete. Orchestrators may attempt
  implementation only after warning that a complete product should not be
  expected.
- `blocked` records stop implementation unless explicit override instructions
  are present.

## ID Families

Ready ids encode the broad authority family first.

`P-*`, `I-*`, `ST-*`, and `SV-*` identify premise, intent, standard, and service
primitives.

`A*` primitives are durable supporting artifacts. They may inform, verify, or
explain the product, but they are not the canonical product promise. Projects
may add a one- or two-letter suffix after `A` for well-understood artifact
types, such as `AR-*` for resources, `AP-*` for proof corpora, `ASN-*` for
snippets, `AD-*` for designs, or `AM-*` for manifests.

`X*` records are flags. They are temporary package-work records, not primitives.
They should close once the required decision, proof, or coordination is
complete, leaving durable results on primitives, artifacts, proof, or history.

## Common Rules

Every primitive record should keep metadata at the top level and type-specific
truth under `fields`. For example, an intent record normally includes:

```yaml
schema: ready/primitive/v1
kind: primitive
id: I-001
class: intent
type: behavior
title: Browse and edit the primitive-backed product spine
status: ready
fields:
  statement: The product promise.
  claim_level: process_proof
  actor: The user, operator, system, or agent this promise serves.
  trigger: The event or state that starts the promise.
  action: The behavior the actor or system performs.
  expected_end_state: The observable state that means the promise was fulfilled.
  value: Why this matters to the user or operator.
  scope: What is included.
  non_scope: What is excluded.
  failure_behavior: What happens when the intent cannot be fulfilled.
  process_decision: Current process choice or proof baseline.
  validation_boundary: What this intent does and does not validate.
  input_types: What the intent reads.
  input_samples: Sample artifact ids for representative inputs.
  output_types: What the intent produces.
  output_samples: Sample artifact ids for representative outputs.
  required_surfaces: Surfaces that must exist for the promise to be inspectable.
  operating_envelope: Runtime or quality limits when behavior can vary.
completion_proof:
  - Observable evidence that proves the intent exists.
refs:
  - role: parent
    id: P-001
artifacts: []
owner_notes: []
```

Put every value an app, compiler, validator, search index, or agent must read in
structured fields. Reader-friendly Markdown, PM summaries, developer briefs,
designer views, and customer-facing docs are compiled from these records.

Do not add a root `summary` field to primitives. If a short summary is genuinely
part of a type's source data, store it under `fields.summary`; otherwise prefer
the type's primary field such as `statement`, `rule`, `role`, `objective`, or
`question`.

Use `owner_notes` only for human product-owner annotations that should travel
with the record without becoming product truth. They are not requirements,
evidence, Completion Proof, or agent instructions. Agents and compilers must
ignore them for satisfaction and enforcement; promote anything authoritative
into typed `fields`, `refs`, `artifacts`, or flags.

## File Rules

- Use one primitive record per source file.
- Use the `.ready.yml` extension for primitive source records.
- Place durable primitives under the configured `ready/product/` directory.
- Place temporary package-work flags under the configured `ready/flags/`
  directory.
- Keep ids stable; paths and titles can change.
- Keep files fragmented and reviewable.
- Store relationships in `refs`, not prose.
- Store structural relationships only. Approved stored roles are `peer`,
  `parent`, and `child`.
- In compact refs, the role describes the target record's position relative
  to the current record: `parent` means the target parents the current
  record; `child` means the target is a child of the current record; `peer`
  means the target is lateral.
- Optional numeric codes are implementation details: `0 = peer`, `1 = parent`,
  and `2 = child`. Source YAML should prefer role strings.
- Do not store semantic verbs such as `serves`, `requires`, `governed by`,
  `refines`, or `interrupts` as ref roles. Reader views derive those labels from
  source kind and class, target kind and class, and structural role.
- Derived relationship labels must include source-to-target and
  target-to-source phrases for the same stored edge.
- Standards govern implementation, behavior, measurement, judgment, or
  maintenance. Attach standards to intents, services, flags, or other standards,
  not to premises.
- Refs may cross Ready kinds when the relationship is valid. For example, a
  flag can use a `peer` ref to a governing standard without becoming a
  primitive.
- Services are implementation dependencies. Attach services to intents or other
  implementation-bearing records, not to premises.
- Do not store an inverse ref for the same assertion. Choose one structural edge
  and let views invert parent/child labels when needed.
- Artifact descriptors only own artifact-to-artifact relationships; product and
  workflow records point to artifacts through `artifacts`.
- Store bulky samples, mockups, screenshots, snippets, and generated outputs as
  artifact payloads referenced by an artifact descriptor id. Raw paths are for
  direct source/file access, not semantic product references.
- Do not store local chat logs, raw provider transcripts, source diffs, secrets,
  or unreviewed scratch content in primitive records.
- Do not store coding lifecycle state on primitives. Use flags for temporary
  readiness, decisions, blockers, discrepancies, and proof gaps.

## Premises

Premises explain why the product should exist. They need a clear statement,
provenance, and linked evidence artifacts. Use top-level `type` to name the
premise role, such as `belief`, `assumption`, `need`, `problem`, `risk`, or
`opportunity`.

Do not store `fields.evidence_confidence` on a premise. Premise confidence is
compiled from linked evidence artifacts, and each evidence artifact must carry
`fields.confidence`. Until the standard defines weighted evidence, calculate
premise confidence as the equal-weight average of linked evidence artifact
confidence values.

Do not use premises for constraints. Constraints, rules, quality bars,
compliance needs, measurement rules, and non-negotiable behavior are `standard`
records.

Do not attach services or standards directly to premises. Premises are
conceptual: they explain why the product should exist and what the product
should respond to. Services and standards apply to implementation-bearing
product commitments. If a service or standard seems related to a premise, attach
it to the intent that serves the premise.

## Intents

Intents are product promises. Use top-level `type` to name the commitment shape,
such as `promise`, `behavior`, `workflow`, `module`, `component`,
`capability`, `experience`, `quality`, `safety`, or `failure`.

Keep `statement` as the concise title-card promise, then decompose the promise
into fields that coding agents can build from: `claim_level`, `actor`,
`trigger`, `action`, `expected_end_state`, `value`, `scope`, `non_scope`,
`failure_behavior`, `process_decision`, `validation_boundary`, `input_types`,
`input_samples`, `output_types`, `output_samples`, `required_surfaces`, and
`operating_envelope`.

Use the decomposition to separate the promise, behavior boundary, data
contract, proof boundary, and operating limits. `statement` should remain
readable prose; it is not the whole implementation contract.

`claim_level` names the strongest claim the current product evidence supports.
Use simple values such as `concept`, `process_proof`, `simulation_baseline`,
`fixture_validated`, `human_labeled_validated`, or `production_monitored`.
This is not Completion Proof. It prevents an intent from implying stronger
validation than the evidence supports.

Required services, governing standards, and intent dependencies belong in
directed `refs`, not duplicate `fields`. Supporting examples and outputs belong
in `input_samples`, `output_samples`, and `artifacts`. Quality-bearing or
live-variable intents must carry an Operating Envelope when behavior can vary
materially after release.

Completion Proof lives on committed intents because it defines how to know the
intended product behavior exists. A flag can carry separate closure criteria
when a temporary package-work record needs to be removed.

## Standards

Standards state rules for implementation, measurement, judgment, behavior, or
maintenance. Use top-level `type` for the standard subtype, such as `rule`,
`constraint`, `quality_bar`, `compliance`, `measurement`, `safety`,
`accessibility`, `proof`, or `implementation_constraint`.

Global standards apply through workspace standard refs; primitive-specific
standards normally apply through `peer` refs on implementation-bearing records
that reader views can label `governed by`. Use `child` standard refs only as
fallback semantics for older trees or when the source intentionally nests a
subordinate standard.

Active standards should not carry deferred or future-only rules. Use a Ready
branch or worktree for a coherent alternate future product, and use a flag on
the current branch only when a decision, blocker, discrepancy, or proof gap
needs attention.

## Services

Services capture dependencies required to build, prove, run, or monitor the
product. Use top-level `type` for the service subtype, such as `platform`,
`api`, `data_store`, `model_provider`, `package`, `runtime`, `worker`,
`deployment`, or `external_system`.

A service should put `role` first, use top-level `status` for service state,
and capture blockers, access expectations, safe credential location, evidence,
proof required, input/output types and samples, implementation instructions,
off-limits work, and failure modes.

## Artifact Primitives

Artifact descriptors are primitives with `class: artifact`. They describe one
artifact purpose with stable ids, provenance, privacy state, usage roles, and
the payload files or safe refs that satisfy that purpose.

The `.ready.yml` record is primitive source. The attached file may be JSON,
JSONL, Markdown, HTML, image, binary, external URL, or safe secret-location
reference; that attached payload is not a primitive unless it also has a
Ready-defined schema or pattern.

Every artifact descriptor must carry `fields.confidence`, `fields.provenance`,
`fields.purpose`, `fields.content`, `fields.intended_use`, and
`fields.format`. File-backed descriptors must give every file a `path` and
`role`.

One descriptor records one artifact purpose. It may list multiple files only
when those files are interchangeable versions or representations of that same
purpose. Files with different purposes, such as buyer inputs, seller inputs,
scoring config, expected scoring outputs, and diagnostics, need separate
descriptors.

`proof_corpus` is the artifact subtype for replayable proof material. Use
`fields` for proof-corpus-specific structure, including `proof_role`,
`authority_level`, `lifecycle_state`, `coverage`, `dataset_contract`,
`oracle_policy`, `replay`, `acceptance_contract`, and `limitations`.

Product and workflow records reference supporting artifacts through their own
`artifacts` lists. Artifact descriptors must not keep reverse product/workflow
links such as `linked_primitives`; artifact descriptors may relate to other
artifacts when one artifact depends on, derives from, includes, or carries the
other.

`type: manifest` under `class: artifact` means the artifact primitive describes
a durable product artifact whose purpose is a manifest or index. It is not the
same as `kind: manifest`, which is structural Ready package metadata.

## Flags

Flags are `kind: flag` records, not primitives. They mark proposed changes,
version divergence, discussion, attention, decisions, blockers, discrepancies,
readiness, and proof gaps without putting lifecycle state into primitive
bodies. A flag identifies intended product truth that is unsatisfied,
unproven, blocked, or disputed; it is not a coding ticket or an implementation
recipe.

Flag records keep `class`, `type`, `compiler_role`, `claimable`, and
`completion_proof` at the top level. Claimability depends on flag class, flag
type, blockers, Completion Proof, and workspace policy. Flags omit `status`
unless they are blocked.
