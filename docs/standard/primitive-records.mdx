# Primitive Records

Primitive records are typed, addressable Ready records. Product-logic
primitives use `.ready.yml` files with `schema: readyroom/primitive/v1`.
Decision/workflow primitives, evidence/artifact primitives, and governance
primitives may use specialized schemas such as `readyroom/flag/v1` or
`readyroom/artifact/v1` when those schemas are the honest contract for the
record.

Humans normally read compiled views in the Ready Room app or docs; the raw file
is optimized for parsing, validation, diffing, and merge review.

## Primitive classes

Use primitive class to describe what kind of authority a record carries:

- `product_logic`: premises, intents, standards, and services that define the
  intended product behavior or constraints for the current Ready branch.
- `decision_workflow`: decision flags, blockers, drift, proof gaps, and
  other workflow or ambiguity records.
- `evidence_artifact`: artifact descriptors for resources, proof corpora, snippets,
  designs, manifests, and handoff material. The record is the primitive; large
  or binary payload files are attachments or safe refs.
- `governance`: authority, process, agent, skill, and template records that
  constrain how product truth may change.

Compiler role matters as much as class. A product-logic primitive states what
should exist; it should not prescribe how to change the current implementation
unless the implementation detail is itself a product constraint. A proof-corpus primitive may be valid
process or regression evidence without being approved product behavior. A
seed-flag primitive may be workflow state without being implementation
permission until its claim gate passes.

## ID families

Ready ids encode the broad authority family first.

`A` primitives are durable supporting content. They include documents, proof
corpora, references, diagrams, reports, snippets, designs, manifests, and other
content that supports product work. They may inform, verify, or explain the
product, but they are not the canonical product intent and they are not active
process state. All artifact descriptors start with `A`. Projects may add a one- or
two-letter class suffix after `A` for well-understood artifact classes, such as
`AR-*` for resources, `AP-*` for proof corpora, `ASN-*` for snippets, `AD-*`
for designs, or `AM-*` for manifests. One-off artifacts may use plain `A-*`.

`X` primitives are temporary process interrupts and proof gates. They exist when
intended product truth needs attention, proof, routing, review, escalation,
approval, correction, or a decision. All flag records start with `X`. Projects may add a
one- or two-letter class suffix after `X` for well-understood flag classes,
such as `XD-*` for decisions, `XS-*` for seed flags, `XC-*` for change flags,
or `XR-*` for resource/readiness flags. An `X` item should close once the
required decision, proof, or coordination is complete, leaving the durable result
on the affected product primitive, artifact, proof, workflow state, or trace.

## Common rules

Every product-logic primitive record should keep metadata at the top level and
type-specific product truth under `fields`. For example, an intent record
normally includes:

```yaml
schema: readyroom/primitive/v1
kind: primitive
id: I-001
type: intent
primitive_class: product_logic
compiler_role: source_truth
title: Browse and edit the primitive-backed product spine
status: active
fields:
  statement: The product promise.
  intent_type: behavior
  claim_level: process_proof
  actor: The user, operator, system, or agent this promise serves.
  trigger: The event or state that starts the promise.
  action: The behavior the actor or system performs.
  expected_end_state: The observable state that means the promise was fulfilled.
  value: Why this matters to the user or operator.
  scope: What is included.
  non_scope: What is excluded.
  failure_behavior: What happens when the intent cannot be fulfilled.
  process_decision: Current process choice or proof baseline.
  validation_boundary: What this intent does and does not validate.
  input_types: What the intent reads.
  input_samples: Sample artifact ids for representative inputs.
  output_types: What the intent produces.
  output_samples: Sample artifact ids for representative outputs.
  required_surfaces: Surfaces that must exist for the promise to be inspectable.
  operating_envelope: Runtime or quality limits when behavior can vary.
completion_proof:
  - Observable evidence that proves the intent exists.
refs:
  - role: parent
    id: P-001
artifacts: []
owner_notes: []
```

Put every value an app, compiler, validator, search index, or agent must read in
structured fields. Reader-friendly Markdown, PM summaries, developer briefs,
designer views, and customer-facing docs are compiled from these records.
Do not add a root `summary` field to primitives. If a short summary is genuinely
part of a type's source data, store it under `fields.summary`; otherwise prefer
the type's primary field such as `statement`, `rule`, `role`, `objective`, or
`question`.

Use `owner_notes` only for human product-owner annotations that should travel
with the record without becoming product truth. They are not requirements,
evidence, Completion Proof, or agent instructions. Agents and compilers must
ignore them for satisfaction and enforcement; promote anything authoritative
into typed `fields`, `refs`, `artifacts`, or flags.

## File rules

- Use one primitive record per source file.
- Use the `.ready.yml` extension for product-logic primitive source records.
- Use specialized Ready schemas for decision/workflow, artifact, or governance
  primitives when a narrower schema preserves a clearer contract.
- Place product-logic records under the configured `ready/product/` directory.
  Place workflow flags under the configured `ready/flags/` directory.
- Keep ids stable; paths and titles can change.
- Keep files fragmented and reviewable.
- Store relationships in `refs`, not prose.
- Store structural relationships only. Approved stored roles are `peer`,
  `parent`, and `child`.
- In compact refs, the role describes the target primitive's position relative
  to the current primitive: `parent` means the target parents the current
  record; `child` means the target is a child of the current record; `peer`
  means the target is lateral.
- Optional numeric codes are implementation details: `0 = peer`, `1 = parent`,
  and `2 = child`. Source YAML should prefer role strings.
- Do not store semantic verbs such as `serves`, `requires`, `governed_by`,
  `refines`, or `questions` as ref roles. Reader views derive those
  labels from source type, target type, and structural role.
- Derived relationship labels must include source-to-target and
  target-to-source phrases for the same stored edge. For example,
  `intent --parent--> premise` renders as the intent `serves` the premise and
  as the premise is `served by` the intent; `intent --child--> service` renders
  as `requires` / `required by`; `intent --child--> intent` renders as
  `fulfilled by` / `fulfills`; `intent --peer--> standard` renders as
  `governed by` / `governs`; `intent --child--> premise` renders as
  `refined by` / `refines`.
- Standards that govern premises, intents, services, flags, or other standards
  should normally be `peer` refs because governance is lateral product truth,
  not decomposition. Child standard refs remain valid fallback semantics for
  older trees or intentionally nested subordinate standards.
- Do not store an inverse ref for the same assertion. Choose one structural edge
  and let views invert parent/child labels when needed.
- Artifact descriptors only own artifact-to-artifact relationships; product and
  workflow records point to artifacts through `artifacts`.
- Store bulky samples, mockups, screenshots, snippets, and generated outputs as
  artifact payloads referenced by an artifact descriptor id. Raw paths are for
  direct source/file access, not semantic product references.
- Do not store local chat logs, raw provider transcripts, source diffs, secrets,
  or unreviewed scratch content in primitive records.
- Do not store coding lifecycle state on product-logic primitives. Seed and
  change flags are decision/workflow primitives that gate coding claims.

## Premises

Premises explain why the product should exist. They need a clear statement,
provenance, and linked evidence artifacts. Do not store
`fields.evidence_confidence` on a premise. Premise confidence is compiled from
linked evidence artifacts, and each evidence artifact must carry
`fields.confidence`. Until the standard defines weighted evidence, calculate
premise confidence as the equal-weight average of linked evidence artifact
confidence values.

Use `fields.premise_type` to name the discovery role. Valid values live in
[`vocabulary.yaml`](../../vocabulary.yaml). Do not use premises for
constraints. Constraints, rules, quality bars, compliance needs, measurement
rules, and non-negotiable behavior are `standard` records.

## Intents

Intents are product promises. Keep `statement` as the concise title-card
promise, then decompose the promise into fields that coding agents can build
from: `intent_type`, `claim_level`, `actor`, `trigger`, `action`,
`expected_end_state`, `value`, `scope`, `non_scope`, `failure_behavior`,
`process_decision`, `validation_boundary`, `input_types`, `input_samples`,
`output_types`, `output_samples`, `required_surfaces`, and
`operating_envelope`.

Use the decomposition to separate the promise, behavior boundary, data
contract, proof boundary, and operating limits. `statement` should remain
readable prose; it is not the whole implementation contract.

`claim_level` names the strongest claim the current product evidence supports.
Use simple values such as `concept`, `process_proof`, `simulation_baseline`,
`fixture_validated`, `human_labeled_validated`, or `production_monitored`.
This is not Completion Proof. It prevents an intent from implying stronger
validation than the evidence supports.

`intent_type` should name the main promise shape. Valid values live in
`vocabulary.yaml`. Use conservative values and rely on `validation_boundary`
when an intent could be mistaken for a stronger claim.

Required services, governing standards, and intent dependencies belong in
directed `refs`, not duplicate `fields`. Supporting examples and outputs belong
in `input_samples`, `output_samples`, and `artifacts`. Quality-bearing or
live-variable intents must carry an Operating Envelope when behavior can vary
materially after release.

Completion Proof lives on committed intents because it defines how to know the
intended product behavior exists. A flag can carry separate closure criteria
when a temporary workflow record needs to be removed.

## Standards

Standards state rules for implementation, measurement, judgment, behavior, or
maintenance. Global standards apply through workspace standard refs; primitive
specific standards normally apply through `peer` refs that reader views can
label `governed by`. Use `child` standard refs only as fallback semantics for
older trees or when the source intentionally nests a subordinate standard.
Active standards should not carry deferred or future-only rules. Use a Ready
branch or worktree for a coherent alternate future product, and use a flag on
the current branch only when a decision, blocker, discrepancy, or proof gap
needs attention.

## Services

Services capture dependencies required to build, prove, run, or monitor the
product. A service should put `role` first, use top-level `status` for service
state, and capture blockers, access expectations, safe credential
location, evidence, proof required, input/output types and samples,
implementation instructions, off-limits work, and failure modes.

## Decision Flags

Decision flags are `readyroom/flag/v1` records with `type: decision`. They hold
product-shaping ambiguity as work that must be resolved, not as loose questions.
Keep the unresolved prompt in `fields.question`, the conservative interim
behavior in `fields.safe_default`, candidate answers in structured
`fields.proposals`, and the decision Definition of Done in top-level
`completion_proof`. When resolved, record the accepted proposal or explicit
deferral, apply or list the resulting primitive edits, flags, actions, or
artifacts, and remove the active decision flag from the open set. History
belongs in Git, events, assessments, or implementation refs.

## Flags

Flags are decision/workflow primitives. They mark attention, blockers, drift,
proof gaps, and coding-readiness state without putting lifecycle state into
product-logic primitive bodies. A flag can be a primitive without being a coding
ticket or implementation recipe; it identifies intended product truth that is
unsatisfied, unproven, blocked, or disputed. Claimability still depends on flag
type, status, blockers, Completion Proof, and workspace policy. Flag records
keep `type`, `claimable`, and `completion_proof` at the top level.

## Artifact Records

Artifact descriptors are evidence/artifact primitives. They describe one
artifact purpose with stable ids, provenance, privacy state, usage roles, and
the payload files or safe refs that satisfy that purpose. The `.ready.yml`
record is primitive source. The attached file may be JSON, JSONL, Markdown,
HTML, image, binary, external URL, or safe secret-location reference; that
attached payload is not a primitive unless it also has a Ready-defined
schema/pattern.

Every artifact descriptor must carry `fields.purpose`, `fields.content`,
`fields.intended_use`, and `fields.format`. Evidence and proof descriptors must
also carry `fields.confidence` and `fields.provenance`. File-backed descriptors
must give every file a `path` and `role`.

One descriptor records one artifact purpose. It may list multiple files only
when those files are interchangeable versions or representations of
that same purpose. Files with different purposes, such as buyer inputs, seller
inputs, scoring config, expected scoring outputs, and diagnostics, need separate
descriptors.

`proof_corpus` is the artifact subtype for replayable proof material. Use
`fields` for proof-corpus-specific structure, including `proof_role`,
`authority_level`, `lifecycle_state`, `coverage`, `dataset_contract`,
`oracle_policy`, `replay`, `acceptance_contract`, and `limitations`. The core
contract is explicit about which descriptor ids are inputs, configs, expected
outputs, diagnostics, generated outputs, and harnesses. A proof corpus can prove
process behavior, fixture conformance, or regression stability; it must not be
cited as human-labeled quality truth, live production validity, or customer
evidence unless those authorities are explicitly present in its fields.

Product and workflow records reference supporting artifacts through their own
`artifacts` lists. Artifact descriptors must not keep reverse product/workflow links
such as `linked_primitives`; artifact descriptors may relate to other artifacts
when one artifact depends on, derives from, includes, or carries the other.
