# Flags and Claims

Flags are `kind: flag` records. They are not primitives, not tickets, and not
implementation instructions. They are temporary bundle-work records for
unresolved Ready-bundle decisions, proposed changes, blockers, discrepancies,
readiness gates, and proof gaps. A completed bundle has no flags.

The intended product state belongs in primitives. A flag should not restate the
whole target spec or prescribe how to change the implementation. It should
identify the unsatisfied or unproven intended state, proposed bundle change,
version divergence, blocker, discrepancy, or proof gap that is keeping the
bundle from being complete, accepted, or safely claimable. It may name blockers
or claim policy and define the observable evidence that removes the flag.

Every flag id starts with `X`. A flag may use a one- or two-letter class suffix
after `X` when the class is well understood: for example `XD-*` for decisions,
`XB-*` for blockers, `XDR-*` for discrepancies, `XR-*` for readiness, or
`XP-*` for proof gaps. Plain `X-*` is valid for arbitrary or one-off workflow
records. Remove a flag once the required process action or decision is
complete; durable results belong on primitives, artifacts, proof, or Git
history.

## Flag Classes

Flags use both `class` and `type`.

- `decision`: product, design, or authority ambiguity affecting bundle truth.
- `blocker`: service, dependency, or authority condition preventing bundle
  completion, proof, or claimability.
- `discrepancy`: implementation, evidence, or view drift from bundle truth.
- `readiness`: seed, change, or discovery gate for bundle claimability.
- `proof`: proof, coverage, or fixture gap in the bundle.

Common types:

- `decision`: `product_choice`, `design_choice`, `authority_choice`.
- `blocker`: `service_access`, `dependency`, `authority`.
- `discrepancy`: `implementation_drift`, `evidence_drift`, `view_drift`.
- `readiness`: `seed`, `change`, `discovery`.
- `proof`: `proof_gap`, `coverage_gap`, `fixture_gap`.

## Claim Gate

A coding agent can claim a flag only when all of these are true:

```yaml
kind: flag
class: readiness
type: seed # or change
compiler_role: workflow_state
blocked_by: []
claimable: true
completion_proof:
  - "User can complete the target workflow in a clean local run."
  - "The relevant verification command passes and the evidence artifact is linked."
fields:
  scope: "The bundle truth this claim builds or proves."
  non_scope: "Work explicitly outside this claim."
  dependencies: []
  required_standards: []
  required_services: []
  required_artifacts: []
  setup: []
  acceptance: []
  blocker_policy: "Claims stop when blockers appear."
```

Claimable flags omit `status`; `claimable: true` means the temporary
bundle-work record can be claimed by a coding agent under the bundle's
governance and authority rules.

Claiming a flag does not mean applying a stored delta. The coding handoff should
give the coding agent the resolved intended product state from `ready/product/`,
plus standards, services, artifacts, intent Completion Proof, and any relevant
flag closure criteria. The coding agent may choose the clean implementation
strategy, including replacement or refactor, inside those product and proof
constraints.

## Decision Flags

Decision flags use `class: decision`. They hold product-shaping ambiguity as
work that must be resolved, not as loose questions. Keep the unresolved prompt
in `fields.question`, the conservative interim behavior in
`fields.safe_default`, candidate answers in structured `fields.proposals`, and
the decision closure criteria in top-level `completion_proof`.

When resolved, record the accepted proposal or explicit deferral, update the
resulting product records, and remove the unresolved decision flag from the
current flag set. History belongs in Git, events, assessments, or
implementation refs; do not preserve desired state as a stored edit list.

## Current Flags

A discovery, seed, or change flag may be sparse. It only needs to point to the
Ready-bundle truth, proposed bundle change, or proof gap that needs attention
and preserve the trigger context. Readiness flags are product/design
responsibility, not coding workflow, until they become claimable.

## Blocked Flags

A seed or change flag may have enough bundle truth and Completion Proof to be
well specified, but still be blocked by a service, authority decision,
environment, or upstream flag. Use `status: blocked` and keep it non-claimable:

```yaml
kind: flag
class: readiness
type: seed
compiler_role: workflow_state
status: blocked
claimable: false
blocked_by:
  - SV-003
completion_proof:
  - "The target workflow is implemented and verified with the linked service stub."
```

This separates the intended product state from the workflow condition that
prevents implementation.

## Completion Proof

Intents carry the durable Completion Proof needed to rebuild the product from
the Ready tree. Readiness and proof flags may also carry top-level
`completion_proof` as closure criteria for the temporary flag: the observable
outcomes and evidence that remove the flag. It must be real proof content, not a
one-word status such as `ready`, `done`, or `complete`, and it does not prescribe
file edits.

## Derived Discrepancies

Apps may derive unstored discrepancies by comparing the current Ready tree to
the codebase, proof artifacts, generated views, assessment snapshots, or prior
Ready commits. Store a flag only when the discrepancy needs durable human
attention, explicit Completion Proof, blocker tracking, claim coordination, or
review history. Otherwise the discrepancy can remain an interpreted app view.
